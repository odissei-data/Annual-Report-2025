Message from the Chair

A year of maturation and commitment

"ODISSEI has grown into an indispensable national infrastructure — one that not only connects data, but connects people, disciplines, and institutions in service of research that matters."

As we reflect on 2025, ODISSEI has continued its mission to be the connective tissue of Dutch social science research. This year has been marked by both consolidation and bold innovation: we have deepened the services we provide, expanded our membership, and — crucially — supported researchers in addressing the complex social questions that define our era.

The submission of the Macroscope proposal, with 31 member organisations already committed to membership through 2031, speaks to the trust the research community places in ODISSEI. This is not merely an administrative milestone — it is a testament to the value of sustained, long-term investment in shared research infrastructure.

In 2025, we saw the maturation of key technical services. The ODISSEI Secure Supercomputer, the Secure Analysis Environment, and the ODISSEI Portal each reached new levels of capability and accessibility. The launch of structural funding for SANE and Firmbackbone ensures that these investments will continue to pay dividends for years to come.

Equally important is the human dimension of our work. The ODISSEI Conference drew over 440 researchers. The Summer School trained a new generation of computational social scientists. The SoDa team provided hands-on methodological support to researchers navigating increasingly complex data environments. We are proud of what ODISSEI has achieved, and look forward to the next chapter — defined by even greater collaboration, openness, and research impact.

Governance

ODISSEI is the Dutch National Infrastructure that brings together sensitive data, computing resources and expertise to drive collaborative social science and economic research. It now consists of 45 member organisations, with 31 already committed to membership through 2031.

Workstream 01 — Data Facility

Access, security,
and infrastructure

Improved data access, secure computing, and technical innovation — expanding dataset availability, improving metadata search and linkage, developing the Secure Supercomputer and SANE environments, and deepening collaborations with CBS and SURF.

  • Expanded CBS services including CBS Data Storage for reusing large datasets requiring substantial computational effort to produce.
  • Microdata Access Grant offered twice, resulting in 12 new projects; introduction of a discount on project start-up costs.
  • CBS launched a self-service portal enhancing user control over projects and microdata use.
  • ODISSEI Knowledge Graph (Proof of Concept) connecting literature, tools, datasets, variables, projects, organisations, and authors.
  • Data Access Broker (DAB) reached Proof of Concept stage, standardising access conditions across SSH data providers.
  • OSSC introduced the TULP pilot — allowing researchers to upload very large datasets with additional safeguards for sharing sensitive data.
  • App Dashboard finalised, replacing the command-line interface and lowering technical barriers to secure environments.
  • SANE matured with 20+ active research projects; SANE and Firmbackbone awarded structural Ministry of Education, Culture and Science funding.

Workstream 02 — Observatory

Data collections
for the long run

Sustaining and developing data collections enabling Dutch researchers to conduct longitudinal, comparative, and population-level social science research — in line with FAIR principles and ethical data practices.

  • LISS panel grant received a record number of applications, doubling awarded projects to fifteen — enabling more free researcher-led data collection.
  • Revised sampling strategy with Statistics Netherlands improved coverage of hard-to-reach population groups.
  • FAIR Implementation Profile for the LISS Data Archive established to improve findability and reusability.
  • Sensitive LISS data accessible through SANE; research project successfully combining location-enriched LISS data with CBS Statline data demonstrates cross-facility linkage.
  • PANL participant recruitment platform entered full production after secured structural funding.
  • Port software enabled structured data collection from Instagram, TikTok, Google, and Facebook.
  • CBG & Ministry of Interior agreed to transcribe personal record cards 1939–1994 via OCR, with IISH standardisation expertise and SANE as disclosure infrastructure.
  • SHARE Wave 10 completed and released; ESS Round 12 collected for the first time using a mixed-mode approach; Dutch Parliamentary Election Study supported.

Workstream 03 — Laboratory

Innovation at
the frontier

Strengthening ODISSEI's capacity for data-intensive social science by advancing secure distributed processing, data donation, population-scale network analysis (PlaNet-NL), and mass online experimentation — with a focus on usability, scalability, and integration.

  • Digital Data Donation Infrastructure (Port): parameters for participant status introduced, pilot with Motivaction completed, iframe integration proposals refined.
  • Restructured Feldspar repository; reusable Python modules and platform-specific scripts consolidated for privacy-preserving data collection.
  • Data donation moved from project-specific implementations to a shared, reusable framework — reducing duplication and improving consistency.
  • PlaNet-NL: Strengthened CBS collaboration, advanced temporal and relational analysis, improved interoperability of large-scale administrative network data.
  • First oTree-based use case for mass online experiments developed and ready for piloting.
  • Moved toward robust experimental designs combining survey, behavioural, and administrative data — increasing integration across all components.

Workstream 04 — Hub

Community,
training, support

The operational centre of ODISSEI — coordinating daily operations, managing grants, and facilitating cross-institutional engagement so that researchers can focus on their work while strategic priorities and operational delivery remain aligned.

  • ODISSEI Conference 2025: over 440 researchers, three hands-on workshops, platform for methodological exchange and interdisciplinary collaboration.
  • 3,100+ followers on LinkedIn and Bluesky; regular newsletter and podcast keeping the community informed of developments and opportunities.
  • SoDa team provided hands-on guidance on methodological, ethical, and legal challenges in working with complex linked data.
  • SoDa Fellowship programme: two application rounds, fostering interdisciplinary collaboration and advanced analytical approaches.
  • ODISSEI-SICSS Summer School trained 24 early-career researchers in infrastructure use and advanced analytical methods.
  • Two SoDa workshops (~25 participants each) on LLM applications, secure analysis in SANE, and community-developed tools.
  • Tutorials and training materials published online, contributing to a growing network of researchers skilled in working with complex data.

Scientific Impact

Research Spotlight

Selected publications enabled by ODISSEI's data infrastructure in 2025, demonstrating the breadth and policy relevance of research conducted using ODISSEI facilities.

02

Labour Economics · Register Data

Wage Premium or Wage Penalty? Gendered Long-term Wage Development of Family Caregivers

Drawing on 19 years of CBS register data combined with retrospectively collected caregiving information (N = 2,659 respondents; 324,940 person-months), this study evaluates hourly wages across caregiving trajectories — distinguishing between never-carers, current carers, and past carers, as well as by duration and intensity. The results reveal that only men benefit from caregiving in terms of wage growth, while women's wage development is slightly weaker after caregiving episodes. Findings challenge both conflict and enrichment theories, highlighting persistent gendered asymmetries in the Dutch labour market.

Raiber, K., Möhring, K., Visser, M., & Verbakel, E. (2026). Work, Employment and Society, 40(1), 3–25.

Future of Work · LISS Panel

Why are employees most susceptible to automation least likely to retrain?

Using novel LISS panel survey data on Dutch employees (N = 1,719), this study investigates the paradox that workers most at risk of automation are least likely to engage in work-related learning. Findings show that these differences are explained by learning intentions and perceived access to education and supportive environments — rather than by subjective perceptions of automation risk. The results inform the design of lifelong learning and active labour market policies in digitising economies.

Jansen, G., Janssen, S., Levels, M., & Fregin, M.-C. (2025). Economic and Industrial Democracy, 0(0).
